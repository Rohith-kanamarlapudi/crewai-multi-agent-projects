The motion before us is that strict laws are necessary to regulate large language models (LLMs). My argument in favor is simple but compelling: without robust regulation, LLMs pose an unacceptable risk to truth, privacy, and democratic stability.

First, consider the proliferation of misinformation. LLMs can generate convincing but entirely false content at scale—from fake news articles to impersonated speeches. In the absence of legal guardrails, malicious actors can weaponize these tools to erode public trust in institutions, manipulate elections, and incite violence. Self-regulation by companies has consistently proven insufficient, as commercial incentives prioritize speed and user engagement over safety.

Second, privacy and consent are being systematically violated. LLMs are trained on vast datasets scraped from the internet, often including personal information, copyrighted material, and proprietary data without permission. Currently, individuals have little recourse when their data is used to train a model that later generates harmful or embarrassing content. Strict laws—modeled on GDPR or similar frameworks—are needed to mandate opt-in consent, transparency in training data, and the right to erasure.

Finally, accountability is absent. When an LLM generates defamatory advice, plagiarizes text, or recommends harmful actions, who is liable? The developer? The user? The cloud provider? Without clear legal liability, victims have no remedy, and developers have no incentive to build responsible systems. Strict regulation would establish clear lines of responsibility, mandatory risk assessments, and forced transparency in model behavior.

Opponents will argue that regulation stifles innovation. But that is a false dichotomy. What truly stifles innovation is public distrust and a landscape of scandals that will inevitably lead to a public backlash and hasty, worse-designed laws later. Preemptive, thoughtful regulation creates a stable framework where innovation can flourish safely. We demand strict laws for cars, pharmaceuticals, and financial products. LLMs are no less powerful—and potentially far more disruptive. The time to act is now, before the harms become irreversible.
